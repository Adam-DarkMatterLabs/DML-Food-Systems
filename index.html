Building an alternative system for school food procurement

A working conversation between the wholesale supply side and the Camden Food Mission surfaced the real mechanics of how school food is bought today — and a clear, workable shape for something better: a single buying group, a shared hub, and guardrails that keep it honest.

Caterlink · sole meals contractor £4.022m/yr · Apr 2025–2028 (+2yr option) Proposed: central buying group + food hub

Verified contract details below are drawn from Camden Council committee reports and Find a Tender notices, current as of December 2025.

01 — What's broken today

One caterer, one long contract — and a pattern worth scrutinising

Camden's school meals aren't bought from a wholesaler and cooked separately by each school — they're produced and delivered end-to-end by a single contracted caterer, Caterlink, under one long-term council contract. That structural fact is verified. What the Browns Lane conversation raised — that this kind of arrangement typically runs on low food quality and hidden margin extraction — is a pattern common to the sector, not a finding proven against this specific contract, and the two should stay clearly separated below.

Verified — the contract

£4.022m / year (£20.11m aggregate)

Caterlink has held Camden's school meals contract since April 2025: a three-year term with an option to extend by up to two further years. It covers 37 primary and 3 secondary schools, roughly 8,000 meals a day, with all primary meals free.

Raised in conversation — the pattern

Unverified for this contract specifically

The Browns Lane conversation described an industry-wide pattern of confidential rebates flowing back to caterers and wholesalers, and quality drifting down wherever a single contractor faces no competitive pressure. This is worth testing against Caterlink's actual performance in Camden — not assumed.

There's a huge level of supply rebate going back to the wholesaler, where all the money is hidden. The margin is passed through, but there's a confidential rebate that comes back. Describing how this kind of arrangement typically operates across the sector — not a specific claim about Caterlink

It's also worth noting the official record currently tells a different story: Camden's own committee reports describe the Caterlink contract as delivering "consistent, high-quality, fresh meals," and the council spent much of 2025 formally exploring insourcing the service before deciding, this December, to let the contract run its term instead. That the review happened at all suggests the quality question was live enough to warrant it — but it stops short of confirming the low-quality, high-margin pattern described at Browns Lane.

Verified

One contractor, no competing bidder

A single caterer holds the whole contract for the length of its term — there is no parallel supplier a school can switch to if quality slips.

Pattern raised

Hidden rebates can set the menu

Where confidential rebates exist, they reward volume and brand deals over nutrition or locality — a known sector dynamic, not yet demonstrated here.

Pattern raised

Local growers have nowhere to plug in

A single, large contract of this kind typically has no mechanism for taking on a community-garden grower or a social enterprise roastery — worth checking directly against Caterlink's current sourcing mix.

Verified

"Security" locks in five years, not flexibility

With extensions, the current contract can run to 2030 — a long window in which the borough's food priorities are tied to one contractor's model.

02 — The logistics insight

Why local salad can cost more than salad from Italy

The clearest, most concrete moment in the conversation was a plain comparison of freight economics. It explains, in one picture, why well-meaning "buy local" efforts keep failing — and what actually needs to change for local growers to compete.

Consolidated import route

~£3,000 / single customs-cleared lorry

One full lorry from Perpignan, cleared once, carrying 24 pallets. The cost per pallet is tiny because the whole run is centralised.

Single local grower pickup

~£300 / one van, one grower

A single van collecting salad from a grower in Devon costs a similar amount per trip — for a fraction of the volume. Local, uncoordinated collection simply isn't economical at that scale.

The fix isn't asking growers to compete on freight — it's changing where the aggregation happens. Ultra-local growers (a King's Cross community garden, for instance) can be collected on short hyperlocal rounds; everything further out needs to be pooled through one distribution point before it reaches any school.

03 — The model that was described

Replace single-contractor dependency with a group Camden controls

Whatever the true balance of quality and margin turns out to be under the current Caterlink contract, the structural risk is already real: one contractor, one long agreement, no competing bidder for the length of its term. The alternative isn't to add more suppliers into the mix — it's a single buying group, owned at council level, that agrees the core ingredient list and sourcing standards. Individual schools would still order freely, but within that agreed frame, and no single contractor sits unchallenged in the middle for five years at a time.

Guardrails set once, above the 68 schools — free ordering underneath, within the agreed list

Inside that frame, three things were repeatedly named as necessary:

  • A shared core ingredient list, agreed for a fixed period (e.g. six months), so it doesn't drift into 68 different requests before it's even started.
  • Brand and sourcing choices made deliberately — regional dairy over a default national brand, organic over conventional — so the buying decision itself does the nutritional and local-economy work.
  • A packing and case-size spec agreed at grower level, so produce arrives ready to distribute without extra handling, damage, or splitting.

04 — Delivery options

Five ways to build the alternative, not mutually exclusive

None of these require inventing new infrastructure from scratch — each was already visible, in some partial form, in the organisations and sites discussed at Browns Lane.

01

A single Camden food buying group

One body — sitting above all 68 schools, co-ops, and the holiday activity fund — agrees the core ingredient list, the sourcing standards, and the wholesale partner(s). Schools keep day-to-day choice, but only within that frame.

Addresses: single-contractor dependency, contract opacity, quality assurance

02

A shared physical food hub

The Camden/Islington-backed site under discussion could house chilled warehousing and distribution for the whole buying group — the same "gal's model" logistics a private organic wholesaler already runs, but owned for civic use.

Addresses: freight economics, cold-chain reliability

03

A hyperlocal aggregation layer

Community gardens and small growers too close to justify full-pallet freight (a King's Cross-scale grower, for instance) are collected on short local rounds and folded into the hub's stock, rather than asked to compete on national freight rates.

Addresses: viability for small/local producers

04

A closed-loop waste and surplus system

Food waste routes to anaerobic digestion through the North London Waste Authority's shared service (with a live "Your Food's Not Rubbish" partnership on the table); genuine surplus already moves to charity redistribution. Both can be built into the hub's standard operating model rather than handled ad hoc.

Addresses: shock resilience, circularity, cost of waste

05

A named-brand pathway for local social enterprises

Rather than each small producer (a prison-training coffee roastery, a market-garden salad grower) chasing its own contract, the hub becomes their single wholesale route to market — provided their ideal customer profile and realistic volume are defined first.

Addresses: route-to-market for values-aligned local suppliers

05 — Why this matters for the Mission

This is the operating system the Camden Food Mission needs underneath it

From ambition to a working buying group

The Mission's aims — better nutrition, more local and organic sourcing, a fairer deal for growers — depend on collective purchasing power existing somewhere Camden actually controls. Right now that power sits with one contracted caterer, Caterlink, for the length of its contract term. Whatever the true quality and margin picture turns out to be, a civic-owned buying group is what turns "we want more organic, nutrient-dense food" from a policy statement into a standing order large enough for growers to plan against — and gives Camden its own visibility into a relationship it currently can't see inside.

It also reframes what "resilience" means for the borough. Depending on a single contractor for the whole school meals service is itself a structural risk — one contract, one set of priorities, one point of failure — regardless of how well that contractor is currently performing. A council with its own coordinated buying group and a network of local growers behind it can respond when supply shocks (economic, climate, or otherwise) hit, rather than being wholly reliant on one contractor's ability to cope.

06 — How it actually gets built

Small, honest cycles — not a single grand launch

The suggested working rhythm was deliberately unglamorous: agree direction, add detail, reconvene, and improve by a small margin each time — rather than waiting for a finished system before starting.

Align on direction

Confirm the shared destination across the Mission, schools, co-ops and suppliers.

Set indicators

Turn the direction into a short list of things that count as "better" — nutrition, local %, rebate-free contracts.

Run the cycle

Operate for roughly three months inside the agreed guardrails.

Reconvene

Share what's improved, what's been learned, and what moves next — then go again.
