Food Futures — dinner & workshop plan

A UBNI-format dinner that confronts guests with three food futures before a single working day turns the one worth choosing into a plan — aligning collective buying power across schools, health, business, community, and citizens.

Tue 5 Aug · Evening dinner experience Wed 6 Aug · Workshop, 10:00–15:00 Hosted by Dark Matter Labs

The hypothesis we're testing

Every borough has a piece of the puzzle. None has the whole picture working together.

Several London boroughs already have parts of a fortified, civic-led food system running well in isolation. The working hypothesis behind this event is that if Camden aligns collective buying capacity across every channel through which food enters the borough — via a Camden Food Buying Group — it can achieve organic, nutrient-dense food at real scale, build a food system genuinely resistant to shocks, and become the first place to prove the whole model, ahead of a wider rollout across London.

The five procurement vectors in scope

Every session in the workshop returns to the same five channels — the idea is to understand, and eventually align, how each one buys food:

School procurement NHS procurement Business & restaurant procurement Food co-ops & food banks Citizen purchasing
This is the collective game that we're all playing — we're interested in the London boroughs that have all got different pieces of the puzzle working, but none of them have the whole picture together. Framing for the dinner's opening remarks

Tuesday 5 August — Evening

The dinner: the UBNI format, as already planned

The dinner runs as a piece of climate-crisis theatre in its own right: guests physically move between three seating scenarios over the course of the meal, each one a different food future, each served and experienced differently. It confronts the room with the fragility of the current food supply chain before anyone is asked to plan an alternative to it.

Three scenarios, three seats, three plates

Participants shift seats between the three scenarios during the meal, so each course is felt at a different table, in a different mode. What arrives on the plate changes with the scenario — from a shot of industrial nutrient paste or plain white bread and butter, through to ration credits guests trade for fresh fruit and vegetables — so the differences are tasted and handled, not just narrated.

Detailing the five vectors

The core working block of the day. Five parallel breakout groups, one per procurement vector, each putting real detail on how alignment gets achieved for that channel — see the breakdown below — closing with a shared reconvene.

Objective: leave at 15:00 with a working draft of actions for each vector, not just a wishlist.

The 3-hour working block, in detail

One breakout per procurement vector

Each group works the same question for its own channel: what would it actually take for this vector to buy through a Camden Food Buying Group, and what's the first concrete step? Groups should include at least one practitioner who already works inside that channel.

Vector 01

Schools

Building on the existing procurement paper: what it takes to move from a single long-term catering contract toward a Camden-controlled buying group with agreed core ingredients and sourcing standards.

Working question: what's the smallest pilot that proves the model without breaking the current contract?

Vector 02

NHS

Where NHS catering and food-related services in Camden already source from, and what alignment with a shared buying group would need from health-sector procurement rules and budgets.

Working question: which NHS sites or services could plug into shared buying first?

Vector 03

Businesses & restaurants

How local businesses and restaurants could buy through the same aggregated supply, and what would make that commercially attractive rather than a compliance ask.

Working question: what's the value proposition that gets a restaurant to switch supplier voluntarily?

Vector 04

Food co-ops & food banks

Where community food co-ops and food banks currently source from, and how routing more of that through a shared buying group changes cost, quality, and reliability for the people they serve.

Working question: does shared buying reduce cost for the co-ops, or just shift where the cost sits?

Vector 05

Citizens

What share of everyday household shopping could realistically route through the same civic-led system, and what that would need — a retail front, a subscription model, a market.

Working question: what's the simplest way a resident actually buys into this?

Reconvene

Pulling it together

Last 20 minutes of the block (14:40–15:00): each group's headline action goes on a shared board, so the day ends at 15:00 with one visible plan across all five vectors rather than five separate ones.

Why this is worth three hours

How the present system weathers a shock, versus a fortified one

This comparison is the pivot point of the day — the case for spending real time on detail, made concrete rather than abstract.

Today — fragmented channels

  • Schools, NHS, businesses, co-ops and citizens each depend on separate, disconnected supply relationships.
  • A shock in one channel (a contract failure, a price spike, a supply gap) has no shared buffer to draw on.
  • Recovery depends on each institution renegotiating alone, from a weaker individual position.

Fortified — aligned buying group

  • Shared demand across all five vectors gives growers and producers a stable, larger, more predictable order to plan against.
  • A shock in one part of the system can be absorbed and redistributed across the wider group rather than breaking one channel alone.
  • The borough negotiates from a position of scale, not from five separate, smaller positions.

What the day needs to produce

Outputs to leave the room with

  • A shared, agreed gap map across the five procurement vectors — not five separate diagnoses.
  • A first-draft action per vector, owned by someone in the room, with a next concrete step.
  • A working case for resilience that can be shown to funders, councillors, and partner boroughs.
  • A clear owner for pulling the five vectors into a single Camden Food Buying Group portfolio plan.
  • A shared understanding, across the boroughs represented, of what a replicable version of this model would need to travel beyond Camden.
